External Hardware

Barcodes

  • Barcodes are used all over the world to keep track of products, items, etc.
  • Barcodes contain and encode data, the alternating black and white sections provide seperation of each character.
  • There are two types of barcode:
    1. Linear Barcode (1D) - vertical stripes of various thickness.
    2. 2D Barcodes - like the Quick Response (QR) code.
  • 2D barcodes encode a larger quantity of data. This means they can store more information, like texts, URLs, or small files

Barcode Use

  • Barcodes are used to allow computers to read the code and extract the information contained within it, in order to input that data into a computer.
  • This is significantly faster than entering it by hand, and far more accurate.
  • They were first used commercially to track train carriages, with detectors at stations and junctions tracking the location of carriages quickly and accurately

Reading Barcodes

  • Laser Scanners:
    • The scanner contains a laser beam which shines through a rotating prism, making it scan rapidly from left-to-right creating a visible red band. The dark bars in the barcode absorb this light, while white spaces reflect it.
    • The scanner detects the intensity of the light reflected back, this generates a waveform that mimics the widths of the bars and spaces in the barcode.
    • The returned data is already in binary form, so reading/converting is very simple and reliable.
    • This is decoded and sent to the computer as a digital signal.

Digital Cameras

  • Digital cameras use a grid of tiny light sensors to measure the amount of light passing through the lens when the shutters are open.
  • Each sensor measures the colour and brightness of each pixel.
  • Each sensor turns the light into electricity and the amount of charge is stored as binary data.
  • The binary data is then recorded onto the camera's memory card.
  • Images can be produced using appropriate image software.

Sensors:

  • A digital camera uses two types of sensors: CCD or CMOS.
  • CCD - couple charged device. Transports tje charge across the chip and reads it at one corner of the array. An ACD turns each pixel's value into a digital value by measuring the charge at each photosite and converting this measurement to binary. Higher quality but more
  • CMOS - complementary metal oxide semiconductor. Use several transistors at each pixel to amplify and move the charge using traditional wires.

Printers

Laser Printers

  • Uses powdered ink, called toner. A computer sends data to be printed and the onboard circuit works out how to print the data.
  • It generates a bitmap of the data. This is printed by a laser beam which scans back and forth across a drum inside the printer, building ip a pattern of static electricity. The static electricity attracts toner onto the page. The ink is then cured by two hot rollers.
  • Advantages:
    • Laser printers are more reliable.
    • Can store more ink and can print faster than inkjet.
    • Higher upfront cost, cheaper ink.

Inkjet

  • Liquid ink is sprayed onto the page in the correct places.
  • Advantages:
    • Can be smaller than laser printers.
    • Cheaper than laser printers.

RFID

Used to identify objects. RFID devices contain coils and a small chip, a reader induces a current into the coil which gives the chip enough power to transmit it's data. There are two types, Active and Passive. In active RFID the chip is powered by a power supply giving it a larger range (300 meters). RFID tags can be written so can be used to store data - which can also be overwritten.

Uses

  • ID, passports
  • Stock control
  • Parts tracing

Secondary Storage

Secondary Storage

  • Used to store data and programs.
  • Non-volatile (data not lost when turned off).

Magnetic Drives

Magnets on platters can be read and flipped by a read/write head, the magnets are laid out in rings, called tracks.

  • Very cheap
  • Slow
  • Less reliable due to moving parts
  • Data can be lost due to EM surges

Optical Disks

Tracks on the disk contain pits and lands, the track is a spiral which is followed by laser. Pits absorb the laser whereas lands reflect them, creating a binary signal which can be read by a photodiode.

  • Portable
  • Widely recognised
  • No internet connection required (compared to streaming)
  • Fragile
  • Most are single write, re-writable disks also only have limited re-writes.
  • Cannot store very large amounts of data
  • Good for the sale of media (movies and music)

Solid State Storage (SSD)

Consist of millions of NAND flash memory cells and a controller that manages pages and blocks and complexities of writing.

  • Based on floating gate transistors that trap and store charge. Each cell is a floating gate transistor
  • A typical flash memory grid has 32 to 256 columns (bit lines), and many rows (word lines). The total grid is named a block, and each row is named a page.
  • Each cell can change its state between charge and no charge.

Advantages/Disadvantages:

  • Smaller and lighter
  • Significantly faster read/write
  • More expensive than hard drives
  • Very energy efficient
  • Much more durable (resistant to shock and vibrations)
  • Silent
  • Much longer lifetime
